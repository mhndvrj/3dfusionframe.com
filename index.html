Abstract

Research Overview

Technical Abstract · MBSH Engineering Private Limited · India · Est. 2000

This paper presents an experimental imaging system designed to enhance depth perception on conventional 2D displays using fusion-conditioned disparity encoding. The system investigates whether pre-conditioned spatial encoding may improve monocular depth perception without requiring dedicated stereoscopic displays or wearable optics.

The Stereopsis 3D Fusion Camera captures scenes through a dual-lens optical system. Through optical fusion within the camera frame medium, it encodes depth information directly into the output signal prior to display. Prototype demonstrations suggest increased scene layering and depth sensation under both binocular and monocular viewing conditions when the output is viewed on standard 2D screens, televisions, or cinema displays.

Initial observations indicate potential applications in visualization, immersive media, medical imaging, and accessibility-oriented imaging research — particularly for viewers with monocular vision conditions. The pre-conditioning of disparity before display reduces dependence on binocular cortical fusion, suggesting a distinct perceptual pathway for depth response.

Note: All claims are based on prototype demonstration. Controlled peer-reviewed experimentation is ongoing. Results presented here are preliminary and investigative in nature. Patent application filed — India & PCT.

Prior Art & Existing Technologies

Understanding the Current Landscape

A rigorous understanding of existing stereoscopic and depth-imaging technologies informs the positioning and design approach of the Stereopsis 3D Fusion Camera system.

01 · Stereoscopic Imaging

Current stereoscopic systems primarily depend on binocular disparity reconstruction within the visual cortex. Left and right eye images are delivered separately — via active shutters, passive polarisation filters, or anaglyph encoding — requiring viewer hardware and bilateral visual function.

02 · Anaglyph Systems

Anaglyph technology encodes left and right channels using complementary colour filters (typically red-cyan or amber-blue). While low-cost and display-agnostic, anaglyph systems produce colour distortion and require viewers to wear filter glasses to decode the disparity signal.

03 · Lenticular & Autostereoscopic Displays

Lenticular lens arrays and parallax barriers enable glasses-free 3D by directing different views to each eye. However, these systems require specialised display hardware and restrict the viewer to narrow positional "sweet spots," limiting practical deployment in uncontrolled environments.

04 · VR / AR Systems

Virtual and augmented reality headsets provide immersive depth experiences through near-eye display optics and head tracking. These systems require wearable hardware, carry significant cost and weight, and present barriers to accessibility for monocular viewers and extended professional use.

05 · Monocular Depth Cues

Monocular depth perception relies on pictorial cues — perspective, occlusion, relative size, shading, and motion parallax — processed within a single visual pathway. Existing imaging systems rarely engineer content specifically to optimise monocular depth cue delivery. The Stereopsis 3D Fusion Camera system explores whether pre-conditioned disparity encoding can serve as an engineered monocular depth cue, detectable prior to cortical binocular fusion.

Theoretical Basis

Perceptual Framework

The system's theoretical basis draws on established principles of binocular disparity, monocular depth cues, spatial layering theory, and perceptual conditioning. The core hypothesis is that fusion-assisted perceptual encoding — performed optically at the camera stage — may deliver a depth signal that is partially detectable before reaching cortical binocular fusion mechanisms.

Rather than framing the system as replacing biological stereopsis, the investigative approach examines whether pre-conditioned disparity presentation creates an enhanced monocular depth response — an observable change in perceived scene layering attributable to the encoding method, accessible to viewers under both binocular and monocular conditions.

Motion perception and temporal depth cues are also considered as contributing factors in video-based demonstrations. The system's two disparity configurations — 100% full-disparity and 25%+25% fusion-blended — allow comparative study of perceptual response across viewing conditions and disparity magnitudes.

Conclusion

Preliminary Findings

The presented system demonstrates the feasibility of fusion-conditioned depth encoding on conventional displays. Preliminary observations suggest enhanced spatial perception under both binocular and monocular viewing conditions across a range of screen sizes and viewing distances.

Further controlled experimentation is required to quantify perceptual behaviour and establish the underlying optical and neurological mechanisms. The Challenge

Limitations of Existing Approaches

Existing 3D camera and display technologies predominantly require specialised screens, glasses, or viewer hardware. Delivering perceivable depth on a standard 2D screen without optical aids remains an active area of investigation.

🕶️

Glasses Always Required

Active or passive glasses are uncomfortable, expensive to maintain, and impractical in professional environments like operating theatres or cockpits.

👁️

Excludes Monocular Viewers

All stereoscopic systems require two functional eyes. Over 45 million people worldwide with monocular vision are entirely excluded from 3D experiences.

🧠

Brain Does All the Work

Fusion happens in the viewer's visual cortex — not the display. This causes eye strain, headaches, and fatigue with prolonged 3D viewing.

📐

Restricted Viewing Angles

Glasses-free autostereoscopic displays demand precise head positioning within a narrow "sweet spot" — impractical for real-world use.

💰

High Infrastructure Cost

Active 3D systems require expensive electronics, battery-powered glasses, and complex synchronisation — all adding cost and failure points.

🔬

Camera-Based 3D on 2D Screen

Delivering stereoscopic 3D directly on a standard 2D screen visible with naked eyes remains an unsolved challenge across existing camera technologies. This is the design objective of the Stereopsis 3D Fusion Camera system.

The Invention

How the Stereopsis 3D Camera Works

The Stereopsis 3D Fusion Camera captures any scene through a dual-lens optical system. Through optical fusion within the camera frame medium, it encodes depth information directly into the output signal — which then plays as true 3D on any ordinary 2D screen, TV, or cinema display. The viewer — with one eye or two — perceives full depth without glasses.

01

Stereopsis Camera Captures Scene

Dual cameras capture the scene as a stereoscopic pair — two offset images with encoded depth information, mimicking the human binocular visual system.

02

Optical Fusion in the Frame Medium

The frame's optical system physically fuses the stereoscopic pair. Fusion is a physical optical process — not neurological — happening before the eye.

03

Depth-Encoded Signal — Standard 2D Screen

The fused depth-encoded output plays on any standard 2D screen or cinema display. No special hardware, no projector modification needed.

04

Any Viewer — Naked Eye — Perceives 3D

With one eye or two, without glasses, the viewer perceives enhanced stereoscopic depth on the ordinary 2D screen — a significant experimental outcome.

🔬 Scientific Insight

Why Our Video Matches the Visual Cortex

The human visual cortex (areas V1, V2, and V3A) processes binocular disparity — the slight difference between what the left and right eye see. fMRI studies confirm that V3A shows remarkable sensitivity to stereoscopic depth stimuli, with cortical activity directly correlated with the amount of perceived disparity.

The Stereopsis 3D Fusion Camera encodes disparity directly into the video signal — before it reaches any screen. The viewer's visual cortex receives a depth-encoded input that activates the same V1/V3A pathways as natural binocular vision. This is why the 3D illusion is genuine — and why it is perceivable even through a single eye, because the depth information is already in the light signal, not waiting to be computed in the brain.

📐 The Science of Viewing Distance

Why Bigger Screen = Stronger 3D

The disparity in our video is encoded as a fixed pixel offset. On a larger screen, the same pixel separation becomes a greater physical distance — increasing the angular difference between what each eye sees, creating a stronger depth signal to the visual cortex.

Rule: Larger screen → wider disparity angle → stronger stereopsis illusion
Why Correct Distance Matters

Research shows comfortable stereopsis requires disparity within 2–3% of screen width for objects in front, 1–2% for objects behind the screen plane. Sitting too close causes excessive disparity and discomfort. Sitting too far weakens the 3D signal.

Ideal: Viewing angle of 30–40° (SMPTE/THX standard) for maximum immersion
👁️
For Single-Eye Viewers

Use Video 1 (100% stereo). Sit at 2–3m from a 55–85″ screen. The high disparity gives the strongest monocular depth cue. Room should be dim.
